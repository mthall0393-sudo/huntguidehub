Wild Hog Hunting in Texas: My Favorite Ranches After 15 Years

I still remember my first Texas hog hunt like it was yesterday. We were driving back to the lodge around 2 AM after a fruitless evening hunt when my guide's radio cracked to life. "Got a group working the south fence line — maybe fifteen pigs." That's when I learned the first rule of Texas hog hunting: these animals don't sleep, and neither will you.

After fifteen years of chasing feral hogs across the Lone Star State — from the brush country of South Texas to the piney woods of East Texas — I've learned that finding the right ranch can make the difference between a freezer full of sausage and a long drive home with nothing to show for it.

Why Texas Still Rules Hog Hunting

Texas has approximately 2-3 million feral hogs, and that number grows by about 20% each year. These aren't your grandfather's farm pigs — they're smart, adaptive, and incredibly reproductive. A single sow can produce two litters of 4-8 piglets annually, and those piglets are reproducing by six months old.

The state's combination of massive private land, year-round seasons, and diverse terrain makes it the undisputed king of hog hunting destinations. But not all ranches are created equal, and after countless hunts, I've narrowed down my favorites.

My Top Texas Hog Hunting Ranches for 2026

1. Rio Grande Hunting Ranch — Clint, TX

This 2,500-acre ranch near Clint has been my go-to for the past five years. What sets them apart is their on-site processing — you leave with your meat already butchered and packaged. The guides know the property intimately, and their night-hunting setup with thermal optics is second to none. Expect to see 40-60 hogs on a good night drive.

What to Expect: The Reality of Texas Hog Hunting

Let me be straight with you: hog hunting in Texas isn't always glamorous. It's early mornings, late nights, and a lot of driving. But when you get it right — when you've got a herd of thirty pigs working through a creek bottom and your guide whispers "take your pick" — there's nothing else like it in hunting.

Here's what most outfitters include:

  • Guided hunt with experienced outfitter
  • Meals and lodging (most ranches are all-inclusive)
  • Processing of harvested hogs
  • Transportation on the ranch
  • Thermal or night-vision equipment for after-dark hunts

My Best Tips for First-Time Hog Hunters

1. Go night hunting at least once. That's when you'll see the most hogs and have the best chances. Day hunts can be productive, but hogs are nocturnal by nature.

2. Bring a quality flashlight and spare batteries. Once you shoot a hog, you'll need to find it in the dark. It's not uncommon to lose pigs in thick cover.

3. Wear quiet clothes. Synthetic blends are louder than cotton. I learned this the hard way when I spooked a herd of twenty because my rain gear crinkled.

4. Bring a cooler for meat. Even with on-site processing, you'll want extra space for chops, tenderloins, and sausage.

5. Book early. The best ranches fill up by February for the spring hunting season.

The Bottom Line

Texas hog hunting offers something you won't find anywhere else in America: almost unlimited opportunity. The ranches above have consistently delivered for me over the years, and I've never left empty-handed when I followed their guidance. The key is choosing an outfit that knows their land and puts you on hogs — not just one that takes your money and hopes for the best.
