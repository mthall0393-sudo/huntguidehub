Best Hog Hunting Ranches in Texas for 2026

When the sun painted the pine‑lined ridges of Central Texas amber, I crouched behind a raised blind, the night‑vision scope humming against my cheek. A low rustle, then a massive boar, lit by the infrared lens, charged past an illuminated bait station. The rush of the night drive at Lone Star Hunting Preserve still rings in my ears – proof that the Lone Star still crowns hog hunters.

Texas remains the premier destination for hog hunting in the United States, offering vast private ranches, disparate habitats, and year‑round opportunities. The following 2026 roundup presents the ranches that blend trophy‑worthy feral hogs, seasoned guide expertise, and comfortable accommodations into a single, unforgettable pursuit.

Why Texas Is the Hog‑Hunter’s Paradise

The Lone Star State shelters over 20,000 square miles of prime hog habitat, from bottom‑land hardwoods to rolling brush country. Warm months stretch the season, while the state’s feral swine management program maintains robust populations, guaranteeing reliable pressure for hunters.

Historical Roots of Texas Hog Hunting

From early 1900s crop‑damage battles to the organized drives of the 1930s, Texas’s relationship with feral swine has been pragmatic and passionate. Post‑World War II motorization turned a necessity into sport; today many ranches preserve that heritage through traditional drive hunts alongside cutting‑edge night‑vision experiences.

Top Ranch Picks for 2026

Our field surveys, landscape scouting, and conversations with guides who have spent decades in the brush narrowed the list to five standout properties.

  1. Lone Star Hunting Preserve – Stephenville, TX
    Nestled on 1,200 acres of oak‑hickory woods and open pasture, Lone Star's bait‑driven drives are a masterclass in night‑time tactics. I arrived in early October and watched a parade of boars cascade across a corn‑baited clearing, illuminated by a thermal scope that cut the darkness like a blade. Their guides excel at night drives, offering thermal optics and high‑lumen headlamps that turn dusk into daylight.
  2. Rio Grande Hunting Ranch – Clint, TX
    Spanning 2,500 acres along the Rio Grande, this ranch blends rugged terrain with river frontage. An annual harvest of roughly 150 hogs yields both daylight and night hunts. Their on‑site processing lab lets you leave with a slab of fresh meat rather than just stories.
  3. Longhorn Outfitters – San Angelo, TX
    Family‑friendly yet serious about harvest, Longhorn provides semi‑luxury cabins, gourmet meals, and a dedicated hog‑control team. Their partnership with Bass Pro Shops ensures hunters have the latest 12‑gauge shells, rugged GPS units, and high‑capacity ammo boxes.
  4. Blackwater Hunting Preserve – Tyler, TX
    Deep in East Texas timber, Blackwater emphasizes sustainable hunting. Working closely with the National Wildlife Federation, they monitor habitat health while delivering consistent harvests.
  5. Hill Country Trophy Ranch – Fredericksburg, TX
    A boutique operation limiting hunters per day, Hill Country offers personalized scouting, night‑vision rentals, and an on‑site meat‑processing lab. A rainy November weekend there taught me the subtle habits of teenage boars and the value of intimate, low‑pressure hunts.

Techniques & Strategies Across Texas Ranches

  • Bait Stations: Corn, soy, or commercial feeds concentrate hogs near hunting zones. Use early‑morning and late‑evening drives to maximize activity.
  • Night Drives: Hogs are most active after dusk. Guides equip hunters with thermal or infrared scopes, spotlights, and high‑lumen headlamps to locate moving boars.
  • Stand‑Up Blinds: Elevated blinds provide clear lines of sight over tall grasses, extending shot opportunities.
  • Spot‑and‑Stalk: In less dense terrain, guides locate a group from a distance then approach on foot, using natural cover to close the gap.

Gear Recommendations for a Texas Hog Hunt

Choosing the right equipment can be the difference between a trophy and a missed opportunity. Below is a starter list that blends tradition with modern tech.

  • Firearm: 12‑gauge shotgun with 3‑inch chambers (buckshot or slugs) or a large‑bore rifle (30‑130 grains) for long‑range shots.
  • Optics: Thermal scope (e.g., Pulsar Accolade) for night hunts, paired with a 10‑12×40 spotting scope for daylight scouting.
  • Protection: Heavy‑duty ear and eye protection, plus a leather harness for shotgun stability.
  • Apparel: Moisture‑wicking layers, camouflage shirts, waterproof boots with ankle support.
  • Additional Gear: Night‑vision goggles, high‑capacity ammunition boxes, reliable GPS, and a personal water‑filtration system.

Seasonal Tips – When to Hunt in Texas

While many ranches offer year‑round hunting, the most productive windows are:

  • October–March: Cooler temps drive activity; night drives are especially effective.
  • Spring (April–May): Young hogs emerge, and bucks become aggressive during the rut.
  • Summer (June–August): Early mornings and evenings are best to avoid heat stress.

Always verify the current license requirements and any county‑specific permits before you travel.

Best Practices for Ethical Hog Hunting

  • Safety Briefing: Attend the pre‑hunt safety meeting and stay within designated zones.
  • Shot Placement: Aim for heart or lungs to ensure a quick, humane kill.
  • Field Dressing: Master basic field‑dressing; many ranches provide on‑site processing stations.
  • Leave No Trace: Pack out all trash, respect wildlife corridors, and avoid damaging water sources.

Conservation & Habitat Management

Responsible hog hunting curtails invasive feral swine that threaten native ecosystems. Many Texas ranches partner with the Texas Parks & Wildlife Department to conduct population surveys and habitat restoration projects. By processing a portion of the harvest, hunters help reduce crop damage, wetland degradation, and competition with native wildlife.

  • Population Monitoring: Rangers use aerial surveys and trail cameras to estimate hog numbers and set sustainable harvest limits.
  • Habitat Restoration: Some outfits invest in replanting native grasses and controlling invasive plants to improve biodiversity.
  • Harvest Reporting: Submit taken numbers to the state wildlife database to inform future management decisions.

Travel & Accommodation Tips

  • Nearest Airports: Dallas/Fort Worth (DFW), Austin (AUS), and San Antonio (SAT) provide rental car services that reach most ranches within 2‑4 hours.
  • Ground Transport: Arrange a shuttle through the ranch or rent a 4‑wheel‑drive vehicle for remote properties.
  • Lodging Options: Choose on‑site cabins, nearby hotels, or campgrounds. Many ranches include meals, though you can also bring your own provisions.
  • What to Pack: Sun protection, insect repellent, a reusable water bottle, and a basic first‑aid kit supplement your hunting gear.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a separate hog permit?
Yes. Texas requires a hunting license plus a specific hog tag in counties that manage feral swine. Check your county’s regulations on the TPWD website.
Can I bring my own firearm?
Most ranches allow personal rifles or shotguns, though caliber restrictions and prior approval may apply. Verify with the outfit ahead of time.
Are there family‑friendly hunting packages?
Absolutely. Ranches such as Longhorn Outfitters and Hill Country Trophy Ranch offer cabin accommodations, kid‑friendly activities, and non‑dangerous “hunt‑the‑hog” simulations for younger participants.
What is the typical cost?
Packages range from $1,200 for a day hunt with basic lodging to $4,500 for multi‑day trips that include meals, processing, and premium gear rentals.

Booking Your Trip

Most ranches accept online reservations through their websites. For a hassle‑free experience, book at least 45 days before the season opens. Many also bundle lodging, meals, and processed meat into comprehensive packages.
