Spring Turkey Hunting Checklist: Everything You Need (2026)

There's a particular kind of darkness that turkey hunters know well — the 4 AM kind, when your alarm shatters sleep and you stumble around in the dark trying to find your boots. I've been there more times than I can count, and in those early morning moments, I've learned to appreciate a good checklist.

Nothing ruins a trip faster than driving three hours to your hunting spot only to realize you left your mouth call at home. Or worse — getting a tom at 40 yards and reaching for a shell that isn't there because you forgot to check your vest. This checklist has saved me more than once.

Licenses & Paperwork

  • ✓ State hunting license
  • ✓ Turkey tag (some states require separate tags for each bird)
  • ✓ Harvest information card (if required by your state)
  • ✓ Federal duck stamp (if hunting public land)
  • ✓ Landowner permission (if hunting private property)
  • ✓ Printed maps of your hunting area (phone GPS isn't reliable everywhere)

Calls & Decoys

The calls debate: I've seen hunters spend $300 on premium calls and do worse than the guy with a $15 box call. My advice? Master one or two calls before buying more. Here's what I carry:

  • Box call: My go-to for yelping. The Wood 'n' Wind Hooter is bulletproof.
  • Slate call: Great for purring and soft yelps. Primos Glass pot is my favorite.
  • Mouth diaphragm: Essential for close-range work. Practice at home!
  • Crow call: Emergency locator — when nothing else works, a crow often gets a tom fired up.
  • Owl hooter: Early morning locator — can pull turkeys from surprising distances.
  • ✓ Hen decoys (2-3 is plenty)
  • ✓ Jake decoy (the aggressive male decoy can either make or break your hunt — use strategically)
  • ✓ Decoy stakes and bag

Weapons & Ammunition

  • ✓ Shotgun (12 or 20 gauge — make sure it's patterned for turkey loads)
  • ✓ Turkey choke (extended, ported chokes are worth the investment)
  • ✓ Turkey loads — #4, #5, or #6 shot (buy a few different brands and pattern your gun)
  • ✓ Extra ammunition (I bring at least 25 shells for a full-day hunt)
  • ✓ Hearing protection (electronic is best)
  • ✓ Shooting sticks or tripod (for steady shooting at longer ranges)
  • ✓ Gun case (for transport to and from the field)

Optics

  • ✓ Binoculars (8x42 is the sweet spot for turkey hunting)
  • ✓ Rangefinder (turkey hunting is often 20-40 yard shots — know your distance)
  • ✓ Red dot or scope (if using a shotgun with mounted optics)

Blinds & Concealment

  • ✓ Popup ground blind (Primos Double Bull or similar)
  • ✓ Blind stakes (the included stakes are often cheap — upgrade to heavy-duty)
  • ✓ Camo netting (optional but helps break up the outline)
  • ✓ Chair or stool (hunting from the ground gets old fast)
  • ✓ Blind bag to carry it all

Clothing

Turkey hunting in the spring means unpredictable weather. It might be 30°F at sunrise and 70°F by noon. Here's what I layer:

  • ✓ Base layer (moisture-wicking synthetic or wool)
  • ✓ Insulating layer (fleece or down vest)
  • ✓ Quiet outer layer (cotton or quiet synthetic — no noisy rain gear!)
  • ✓ Rain jacket (spring storms happen fast)
  • ✓ Baseball cap (camo pattern matches most setups)
  • ✓ Face mask and gloves (spring mornings are cold)
  • ✓ Comfortable boots (broken in before your hunt!)
  • ✓ Extra socks

Essential Accessories

  • ✓ Hunting vest with lots of pockets
  • ✓ Water bottle (staying hydrated matters)
  • ✓ Snacks (granola bars, beef jerky — something to keep your energy up)
  • ✓ Knife (sharp — you'll need it for field dressing)
  • ✓ Gloves (rubber for handling meat, warm for cold mornings)
  • ✓ Headlamp (for getting in and out in the dark)
  • ✓ Cell phone (for emergencies, but keep it on silent)
  • ✓ Scent eliminator (spring rains can make scents tricky)
  • ✓ Hand warmers and toe warmers (just in case)
  • ✓ Tissue or toilet paper (trust me on this one)

Post-Hunt Essentials

  • ✓ Game bag (for carrying the bird out)
  • ✓ Cooler with ice (for warm days)
  • ✓ Plastic bags for feathers/wings (if saving for taxidermy)
  • ✓ Camera (for the trophy shot!)

Pre-Hunt Week Checklist

A week before your hunt, run through this routine:

  • Check your equipment — fire every call, test your loads at the range
  • Pattern your shotgun with your chosen loads (shoot at least 10 shots at the distance you'll likely shoot)
  • Check all licenses and tags — don't assume they're valid
  • Charge all batteries (electronics, headlamp, rangefinder)
  • Break in your boots (if they're new)
  • Check the weather forecast and adjust layering accordingly
  • Scout your location if possible

Final Thoughts

The best turkey hunters are the most prepared. That means having the right gear, being in the right place, and having your head in the game. A good checklist takes the stress out of the pre-dawn rush and lets you focus on what matters: hearing that first gobble cut the morning air.

Now get out there — and make some memories in the spring woods.
