How to Choose the Right Hunting Guide: 7 Questions That'll Save You Hundreds

I once paid $3,200 for a five-day elk hunt in Colorado that turned into a expensive camping trip. The outfitter was nice enough, but we saw exactly zero elk, and he spent more time talking about his "proven methods" than actually putting me on animals. That $3,200 taught me more about choosing guides than any other experience in fifteen years of hunting.

Here's the truth the hunting industry doesn't want you to know: the most expensive outfitter is rarely the best, and the cheapest is almost never a bargain. What matters is asking the right questions before you book. After blowing it on that Colorado hunt, I developed a system that hasn't let me down since.

The 7 Questions That Matter Most

Before you book any guided hunt, get the outfitter on the phone and ask these questions. If they dodge or give vague answers, move on. Here's what to listen for:

1. "What's your actual success rate — and can I talk to recent clients?"

This is question number one, and it separates professionals from amateurs. A good outfitter will happily connect you with 2-3 hunters who hunted with them in the last 12 months. Be specific: ask about the time of year they hunted and whether they were successful. Success rates vary wildly by season — an outfitter might have a 90% success rate in November but only 30% in October.

2. "What's included in the price — and what costs extra?"

This is where things get expensive. I've booked hunts that quoted $2,500 only to discover at the end that trophy fees, tag processing, alcohol, and tip money added another $1,200. Get everything in writing before you put down a deposit. Ask specifically about: license fees, tag costs, trophy fees, alcohol, guide tips, and any hidden surcharges.

3. "How many hunters per guide — and will I have a dedicated guide?"

Some outfitters pack four hunters per guide to maximize profit. Others give you a private guide. The difference in experience is enormous. A one-on-one guide can tailor the hunt to your abilities, take you exactly where the animals are, and won't waste time managing other hunters. It's worth paying more for.

4. "What's your land situation — private, public, or both?"

This matters more than most hunters realize. Private land outfitters generally have less hunting pressure and better control over their operation. Outfitters running hunts on public land have to deal with other hunters, and success can vary wildly depending on who shows up. Some outfitters use both — ask which you'll primarily be hunting.

5. "What happens if we don't see anything? What's your policy?"

This is the question that separates good outfitters from bad ones. Professional operations often offer discounted return trips, partial refunds, or at minimum, a clear policy for bad weather or poor conditions. If they say "that's just how hunting goes" — that's a red flag. Good outfitters stand behind their product.

6. "What's the typical group size and hunter experience levels?"

I've been on hunts where I'm the only serious hunter in a group of four guys who hadn't fired a rifle in ten years. The guide naturally spent most of his time helping beginners, which meant less attention for me. Ask whether you'll be hunting with experienced hunters or beginners, and how the guide balances the group.

7. "How do you handle meat and trophies?"

For big-game hunts, this is critical. Ask whether meat processing is included, who handles the trophy (taxidermy), and whether they can arrange shipping. Some outfitters have on-site processors; others leave you figuring it out. Getting a 200-pound elk back home isn't cheap — factor these costs in.

Red Flags That Should Make You Run

No references or testimonials: Every legitimate outfitter should have happy clients who'll vouch for them.

Vague pricing: "It depends" usually means they're hiding something or raising prices based on how much they think you can pay.

No written contract: Get everything in writing. Verbal agreements mean nothing when things go wrong.

Pushy sales tactics: Good outfitters don't need to pressure you. If they're pushing hard to book now, they probably have something to hide.

No presence online: In 2026, every legitimate business has a website and reviews. Nothing? That's suspicious.

What I've Learned: The Price Sweet Spot

After 30+ guided hunts, I've found the sweet spot is usually mid-range — not the cheapest option, not the most expensive trophy ranch. Here's my rule of thumb:

  • Deer hunting: $1,500-$3,000 for a quality guided hunt
  • Elk hunting: $3,500-$6,000 for a legitimate guided hunt
  • Turkey hunting: $800-$1,500 for a multi-day guided hunt
  • Waterfowl: $500-$1,500 for a multi-day guided hunt

Anything significantly below these ranges, and you're probably dealing with an outfitter who can't fill their calendar — which usually means something is wrong. Way above these ranges, and you're paying for fancy lodging, not better hunting.

The Bottom Line

Choosing the right hunting guide comes down to doing your homework before you book. The few minutes you spend asking these questions can save you hundreds — or even thousands — of dollars and a week of frustration. Don't be afraid to ask hard questions. Professional outfitters welcome them, and anyone who gets defensive is telling you something important about how they do business.
