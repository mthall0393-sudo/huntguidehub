Antelope Hunting: The Draw, the Guides, and Everything Else

Pronghorn antelope are the fastest land animal in North America, and they're also one of the most challenging trophies to hunt. After three years of applying and finally drawing a Wyoming tag, then spending a week chasing antelope across the sage sea, I can tell you — these animals are worth the effort.

Here's everything I wish I'd known before starting my antelope journey.

Understanding the Draw System

Antelope tags are tougher to draw than elk in most states. Here's the breakdown:

Wyoming

The best state for antelope. Non-resident draw odds are actually reasonable — about 15-25% for most units if you have 2-3 points. The quality is incredible, with some of the biggest horns in the country. Apply early and be patient.

Montana

Good populations and decent draw odds, though they're tightening. The eastern Montana units hold great numbers of animals. Look at units around Malta, Glasgow, and the Crazies.

Nebraska

Overlooked by many hunters, Nebraska offers excellent antelope hunting with relatively easy draw odds. The sandhills terrain is beautiful, and the animals are typically in great shape.

New Mexico

The hardest state to draw for non-residents, but the quality is top-tier. If you've got points saved up, this is the place for a true trophy.

Colorado

Limited tags, mostly in the northwest. The animals aren't as big as Wyoming but the scenery is unmatched.

When to Apply

Most states have spring application deadlines — typically late winter or early spring. Start researching in January, get your applications in by March, and hope for the best. Building preference points takes time, so apply even in years you don't plan to hunt.

Finding a Guide

Antelope are notoriously difficult to hunt without a guide. They're incredibly keen-sighted, can run at 60+ mph, and will spot you from a mile away. A good guide knows the terrain, has glassing experience, and can get you within range.

Guide costs are typically $2,500-4,500 for a 5-day hunt. Many elk outfitters also run antelope hunts, so start with your elk guide — they probably have antelope contacts.

What Makes Antelope Different

  • Glass, glass, glass: You'll spend 80% of your time behind binoculars looking for animals, 20% actually hunting
  • Wide-open terrain: No trees to hide in. You're trying to spot them before they spot you
  • Speed: If they bolt, they're gone. Take your shot when you have the chance
  • Water holes: In hot weather, water is the key. Find the water, find the antelope
  • Long shots: Antelope are hunted at longer ranges than any other big game — practice at 300+ yards

The Bottom Line

Antelope hunting requires patience — both in building points for tags and in the field. But there's nothing quite like a trophy buck standing on a ridge, black horns catching the light, looking at you from 400 yards away. It's one of the most rewarding hunts in North America.

Start applying. Be patient. And when your number finally comes up, hire a guide. It'll be worth every penny.
