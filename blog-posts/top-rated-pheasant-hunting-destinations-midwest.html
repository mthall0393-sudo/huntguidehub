Pheasant Hunting in the Midwest: The Complete 2026 Guide

I've killed a lot of roosters over the years — in South Dakota, Kansas, Nebraska, North Dakota, you name it. But nothing compares to that first morning in South Dakota, when I watched a rooster explode from the CRP field like a rocket, heard my Lab barking in the distance, and realized why people drive thousands of miles every fall for this.

Here's what I've learned about hunting ringnecks in the Midwest after two decades of chasing them.

Why the Midwest?

The Midwest is the heart of American pheasant hunting for good reason. The combination of fertile farmland, Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) fields, mild weather, and a decades-long tradition of bird hunting has created the most accessible and productive pheasant habitat in the country.

You can drive to most Midwestern pheasant destinations from anywhere in the US, book a hunt for $300-500, and be in the field the next morning. Compare that to booking a sheep hunt in Alaska or a elk hunt in Colorado, and you see why pheasant hunting is the gateway drug of big game hunting.

My State Rankings

1. South Dakota

The undisputed king of pheasant hunting. The combination of public land access (walk-in acres program), incredible bird numbers, and a culture built around hunting makes South Dakota first on my list. The pheasant population has dipped in recent years due to habitat loss and weather, but it's still the best bet for most hunters. The key is hitting it right — early October before the heavy hunting pressure moves birds.

Best units: Mitchell, Gregory, Brule County • Cost: $400-800/day

2. Kansas

Kansas is the dark horse of pheasant hunting, and honestly, I think it's still a bit underrated. The Flinthills region around Manhattan and Emporia offers incredible tall grass habitat, and the bird numbers have been strong in recent years. What's great about Kansas is the value — it's significantly cheaper than South Dakota, the public land is underutilized, and the birds are there.

Best units: Flint Hills, Smoky Hills • Cost: $300-600/day

3. Nebraska

Nebraska doesn't get as much attention as its neighbors, but the Sandhills region is incredible habitat. The public land access is excellent, bird numbers are strong, and there's less pressure than South Dakota. The terrain is wilder — more bluffs and creek bottoms — which makes for a more challenging hunt but also more rewarding.

Best units: Valentine, Ainsworth, Broken Bow • Cost: $350-650/day

4. North Dakota

North Dakota has had some tough years with avian influenza and habitat loss, but the pheasant hunting can still be excellent, particularly in the southwest. The key is finding the right guide or hunting public land effectively. It's more remote than the other states, which keeps pressure lower, but also means fewer amenities.

Best units: Dickinson, Medora, Bowman • Cost: $400-700/day

5. Iowa

Iowa has gone through some rough patches with bird flu and habitat decline, but the Pheasants Forever organization is headquartered there, and the conservation ethic runs deep. Public land is limited but well-managed. The main challenge is the limited public access — you'll either need a guide with private land access or be willing to knock on doors.

Best units: Rathbun Lake area, Lucas County • Cost: $400-750/day

What to Expect on a Pheasant Hunt

A typical day starts early — be in the field by first light. You'll walk a grid pattern through fields, with your dog working ahead. When a bird flushes, it's like a bomb going off — explosive flight, rapid wingbeats, and then suddenly it's gone over the horizon.

What to bring:

  • A good flushing dog or pointing dog (labs, pointers, setters)
  • 12-gauge shotgun with improved cylinder or modified choke
  • Size 5 or 6 shot is the sweet spot
  • Realistic expectations — you'll miss more than you hit!

The Bottom Line

If you've never been pheasant hunting, put it on your bucket list. It's the most accessible, affordable, and satisfying wing-shooting in America. And if you've been hunting roosters for years, you already know — there's nothing like the sound of a flushing pheasant and a good dog working the heavy cover.

Get after it this fall. Your dog will thank you.
