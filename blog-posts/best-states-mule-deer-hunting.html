Best States for Mule Deer Hunting: My Rankings After 20 Years

There's a moment every mule deer hunter knows — you're glassing a distant hillside when a big buck steps out from behind a juniper, ears perked, looking right at you. That moment — the jaw-dropping recognition that you've just seen a true giant — is why we do this. After twenty years of chasing mulies from Mexico to Canada, I've learned that not all states are created equal when it comes to producing truly world-class deer.

I've been lucky enough to hunt mule deer in Colorado, Wyoming, Montana, Idaho, Utah, New Mexico, Arizona, and Nevada. Here's my honest take on each state — the good, the bad, and the reality of what's out there today.

My State Rankings

1. Colorado — The Gold Standard

Colorado remains the king of mule deer states, and it's not close. The combination of quality public land, healthy deer populations, and the highest concentration of trophy-class bucks in the country makes it #1. The units around Craig, Meeker, and the San Juan country consistently produce deer in the 180+ class.

Draw odds: Difficult for non-residents. Expect to apply for 5-10 years before drawing a prime unit. Over-the-counter (OTC) licenses exist but in lower-quality units.

Cost: $200-500 for non-resident license. Guide costs $3,000-8,000 for a quality outfitter.

Best months: October (rifle), September (archery)

2. Wyoming — The Dark Horse

Wyoming is my favorite state for the money. The units in the northwest (Dubois, Cody, Jackson region) hold incredible numbers of mule deer, and while the big ones aren't as common as Colorado, the odds of seeing a mature buck are higher because of the population density. The weather can be brutal, but that's part of the experience.

Draw odds: Better than Colorado for non-residents, especially for leftover tags. Some OTC archery in September.

Cost: $200-400 for license. Guides typically $3,500-6,000.

Best months: October (general), September (archery)

3. Montana — The Sleepin' Giant

Montana gets overlooked because the elk hunting is so famous, but the mule deer in some units are absolutely stellar. The areas around Glasgow, Malta, and the Missouri Breaks hold remarkable numbers of deer, and the state has made real strides in managing for older age classes. What's refreshing is the relatively easy draw odds compared to Colorado.

Draw odds: Much easier than Colorado or Wyoming. Some excellent leftover tags available.

Cost: $250-350 for non-resident license. Guides $3,000-5,500.

Best months: October (rifle), November (muzzleloader)

4. Idaho — The Value Pick

Idaho is the best value in the West for serious mule deer hunters. The units in the eastern part of the state (the Bear Lake area, Gem Valley, the Pahute) produce genuine giants, and the draw odds are far easier than the big three. The infrastructure isn't as developed as Colorado or Wyoming, which keeps prices down and pressure lower.

Draw odds: Very reasonable for most units. Good leftover tag availability.

Cost: $200-300 for license. Guides $2,500-4,500.

Best months: October (general), September (archery)

5. Utah — The Trophy Gamble

Utah is a fascinating state for mule deer — the genetics are there, and the state produces some absolutely massive deer, particularly in the Book Cliffs and Henry Mountains. The challenge is the draw system, which heavily favors applicants who've built up preference points over years. If you've got points saved up, Utah can be incredible. If you're starting from zero, it's a tough state to crack.

Draw odds: Very difficult without points. Premium units require 10+ years of points.

Cost: $250-400 for license. Guides $4,000-8,000.

Best months: October (general), September (archery)

6. New Mexico — Underrated Gem

New Mexico doesn't get enough love. The GMUs in the northwest corner of the state (Chama, Cuba, etc.) hold excellent deer, and the draw is far easier than Colorado or Utah. The big challenge is the terrain — it's serious country, with deep canyons and thick cover. You won't see as many deer from the road, but the ones you find can be dandy.

Draw odds: Reasonable for most units.

Cost: $150-250 for license. Guides $2,500-4,500.

Best months: October (general), November (muzzleloader)

7-8. Arizona & Nevada — The Specialists

Both states produce genuine giants and attract serious hunters, but they're niche choices. Arizona requires massive preference points for the best units. Nevada has enormous terrain but relatively low deer density — you can go weeks without seeing another hunter but also struggle to locate deer. Both are worth hunting if you have a specific tag, but not the first places I'd recommend for someone starting out.

The Bottom Line

If you're just starting your mule deer journey, here's my advice:

  • For the best odds: Start with Montana or Idaho — you'll see deer, you'll have a realistic shot, and you won't need a decade of points to get in.
  • For the biggest bucks: Put in for Colorado every year and be patient. The odds are long, but when it pays off, it's worth it.
  • For the best value: Idaho or New Mexico — you can hunt year after year without breaking the bank.
  • For the experience: Wyoming — the hunting is great, the scenery is unmatched, and you'll come home with stories regardless of whether you tag out.

The truth is, every state on this list has produced 200+ class mule deer. The difference is in your odds, your budget, and how much patience you have. Pick the one that matches your situation and get after it.
